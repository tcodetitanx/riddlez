RIDDLE 2E1: The Paradoxical Duel
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
Two duelists (A and B) will each choose a number from 1-10. If they choose the same
number, nobody wins. Otherwise, the person who chose the LOWER number wins... UNLESS
the other chose exactly one higher, in which case the HIGHER number wins.

(Example: 3 beats 5, but 4 beats 3. Same with 7 vs 8, etc.)

THE QUESTION: In Nash equilibrium, what strategy should each duelist use?

HINT: This is related to Rock-Paper-Scissors with 10 options.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Winning rules: Lower wins unless loser chose exactly 1 less.
        - 1 beats 3,4,5,6,7,8,9,10 (not 2)
        - 2 beats 1 (special) and beats 4,5,6,7,8,9,10 (not 3)
        - 3 beats 2 (special) and beats 5,6,7,8,9,10 (not 4)
        - ...
        - 10 beats 9 (special) only. Loses to everything else.

Step 2: Let's count wins for each number against uniform opponent:
        - 1: beats 3-10 (8 numbers), loses to 2. Wins 8/9 = 89%
        - 2: beats 1, 4-10 (8 numbers), loses to 3. Wins 8/9 = 89%
        - ...
        - 9: beats 8, loses to 1-7, 10 beats via special? Wait.

Let me recalculate:
        - 9: beats 8 (special). Loses to 1-7. What about 10?
          9 vs 10: lower is 9. But 10 is exactly 1 higher. So 10 wins.
          9 loses to 10 too. 9 beats only 8. Wins 1/9 = 11%.
        - 10: beats 9 (special). Loses to 1-8. Wins 1/9 = 11%.

Step 3: Lower numbers are much better! 1 and 2 are best.
        But wait - if everyone plays 1, ties happen. If you play 2 against 1, you win!

Step 4: Pure strategy equilibrium? If A plays 1, B plays 2 to beat 1.
        If B plays 2, A plays 3 to beat 2. If A plays 3, B plays 4... cycle!

Step 5: Mixed strategy equilibrium: Need equal expected payoff for all strategies played.
        This is complex, but intuitively: play low numbers more often, with some mixing.

SIMPLIFIED ANSWER: Mix primarily between 1 and 2.
- If opponent plays 1: you want 2.
- If opponent plays 2: you want 3.
- If opponent mixes 1,2: you want 2 (beats 1) or 3 (beats 2).

Symmetric equilibrium: Each plays 1 and 2 with equal probability.
- P(1 vs 1) = 0.25: tie
- P(1 vs 2) = 0.25: B wins
- P(2 vs 1) = 0.25: A wins
- P(2 vs 2) = 0.25: tie
Expected payoff: 0 for each. Equilibrium!

ANSWER: Each mixes uniformly between 1 and 2.

VERIFICATION:
- Against (50% 1, 50% 2): Choosing 1 gives 50% win (vs 2? No, 2 beats 1), 50% tie.
  Wait, 2 beats 1 via special rule (exactly 1 higher).
  So 1 vs 2 → 2 wins. 1 never wins against (1,2) mix. 1 gives: 50% tie, 50% loss.
- Choosing 2: vs 1 → win, vs 2 → tie. 2 gives: 50% win, 50% tie.
- 2 dominates 1! So pure 2 is better.

NEW ANALYSIS: Against pure 2, play 3 (beats 2). Against 3, play 4. Cycle up to 10.
Against 10, play 1 (1 beats 10). Full cycle!

ACTUAL EQUILIBRIUM: This is a generalized RPS. Mix over all numbers? Complex.

I'll acknowledge: Full solution requires solving the cyclic game matrix numerically.

RIDDLE 2E2: The Recursive Election
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
Three candidates (A, B, C) run for election. 99 voters will vote. Each voter's
preference is determined by their PREDICTION of who will win:

- Voters 1-33 will vote for whoever they predict will LOSE.
- Voters 34-66 will vote for whoever they predict will WIN.
- Voters 67-99 will vote for the candidate they predict will come in SECOND.

All voters are perfect predictors (they accurately predict the final result, then
vote according to their rule).

THE QUESTION: Who wins?

HINT: Consider fixed points.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Let the final result be A wins, B second, C third.
        - Voters 1-33 vote for C (predicted loser). 33 votes for C.
        - Voters 34-66 vote for A (predicted winner). 33 votes for A.
        - Voters 67-99 vote for B (predicted second). 33 votes for B.
        Result: A=33, B=33, C=33. THREE-WAY TIE. Nobody wins! Contradiction.

Step 2: Try: A wins, B and C tied for second.
        - Voters 1-33 vote for B or C (both "lose" equally?). Unclear.
        - This case is ambiguous.

Step 3: Try: All three tied.
        - Voters 1-33 vote for... any (all tied). Unclear.
        - Voters 34-66 vote for any (all tied). Unclear.
        - Voters 67-99 vote for any (all tied).
        If votes split evenly: 33 each. TIE confirmed!
        Self-consistent? If outcome is tie, and each group splits 11-11-11, then
        A=B=C=33. Yes, tie is self-consistent.

Step 4: Is there a NON-tie equilibrium?
        Suppose A wins strictly: A > B ≥ C.
        - Voters 1-33 vote for loser (C). C gets 33.
        - Voters 34-66 vote for winner (A). A gets 33.
        - Voters 67-99 vote for second (B). B gets 33.
        Result: A=33, B=33, C=33. TIE. Contradicts "A wins strictly."

Step 5: Any strict ordering leads to tie (each group votes for a different candidate).
        The only stable outcome is a three-way tie.

ANSWER: Three-way tie (33 votes each).

VERIFICATION:
- If outcome is tie, each "prediction" is ambiguous (no clear winner/loser/second).
- Reasonable interpretation: votes split evenly within each group.
- 33 voters × 3 groups = 99 votes. 33 each = confirmed tie.
- If any strict ordering is predicted, votes split 33-33-33, forcing tie.
- Self-fulfilling tie! ✓

RIDDLE 2E3: The Infinite Regress Auction
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
An item worth $100 to each of two bidders is auctioned. The rule: each bidder's
bid must be exactly TWICE the other bidder's bid.

THE QUESTION: What happens?

HINT: Can both rules be satisfied?

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Let A bid x, B bid y.
        Rule: x = 2y AND y = 2x.
        Substituting: x = 2(2x) = 4x → 3x = 0 → x = 0.
        Then y = 2(0) = 0.

Step 2: Both bid $0. Does this satisfy the rule?
        - A's bid (0) must be twice B's bid (0): 0 = 2(0) = 0. ✓
        - B's bid (0) must be twice A's bid (0): 0 = 2(0) = 0. ✓

Step 3: Both bid $0. The item goes to... nobody? Or random winner?
        If random winner at $0, expected payoff = $50 each.

Step 4: Can either deviate?
        If A bids $1, rule says B must bid $2 (to be twice A's bid).
        But then A must bid $4 (twice B's $2). Contradiction!
        No bid except $0 satisfies both constraints.

ANSWER: Both bid $0. The item is won for free (by tiebreaker or split).

INSIGHT: Self-referential constraints often collapse to trivial solutions.

VERIFICATION:
- (0, 0): 0 = 2×0 ✓, 0 = 2×0 ✓
- Any other pair: x = 2y and y = 2x → x = 0, y = 0. Only solution. ✓
