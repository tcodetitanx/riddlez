RIDDLE 2A1: The Self-Fulfilling Survey
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
A pollster asks 1000 people: "Will more than 500 people answer 'Yes' to this question?"
Each person answers privately. Each person is told: "Your answer doesn't affect the
outcome - we've already surveyed the other 999 people."

But here's the catch: everyone is told this simultaneously. No one's answer is
actually fixed yet.

THE QUESTION: If everyone reasons rationally about what to answer, what happens?

HINT: Consider what "rational" means when your answer depends on others' answers.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Each person's rational answer depends on their belief about the final count.
Step 2: If I believe >500 will say Yes, my rational answer is... to agree? Let's see.
        - If I answer "Yes" (expecting >500 Yes), I'm predicting correctly IF >500 say Yes.
        - But my goal isn't just to predict correctly - I'm ASKED what I PREDICT.
        - If I predict "Yes" and I'm among the >500, I'm correct.

Step 3: Symmetric equilibrium: If everyone says "Yes," count = 1000 > 500.
        Prediction "Yes" is correct. But they predicted >500 would say YES to the question.
        Wait, the question asks if >500 will answer "Yes." If all answer "Yes," yes >500 did.
        So answering "Yes" = predicting "Yes" which is correct. ✓

Step 4: Alternative equilibrium: If everyone says "No," count = 0 < 500.
        Prediction "No" is correct (not more than 500 said Yes). ✓

Step 5: BOTH are stable equilibria!
        - All-Yes: Everyone predicts >500 say Yes, 1000 say Yes, prediction correct.
        - All-No: Everyone predicts ≤500 say Yes, 0 say Yes, prediction correct.

Step 6: Mixed: If 500 say Yes, 500 say No. Those who said Yes are wrong. Those who
        said No are correct. Not stable (Yes-sayers want to switch).

ANSWER: Two equilibria exist: unanimous "Yes" or unanimous "No."
The question creates its own answer through collective belief.

VERIFICATION:
- All Yes: 1000 Yes, prediction ">500 will say Yes" is true. ✓
- All No: 0 Yes, prediction ">500 will say Yes" is false, but they answered No. ✓
- Mixed at 600/400: 600 answered Yes, 400 answered No.
  - 600 predicted >500 Yes: correct!
  - 400 predicted ≤500 Yes: wrong!
  - The 400 should switch to Yes. Not stable.

ARCHETYPE SCORES:
- Originality: 9/10 (novel self-referential survey)
- Cleverness: 9/10 (dual equilibrium is elegant)
- Simplicity: 8/10 (clear setup)
- Solution Success: 9/10 (complete equilibrium analysis)

TOTAL: 35/40 ★

RIDDLE 2A2: The Quantum Oracle
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
An oracle answers questions about probability. You ask: "What's the probability
that your next statement will be true?"

The oracle always answers with a probability between 0 and 1, and her accuracy
matches her stated probability over many trials.

THE QUESTION: What number does she say?

HINT: What happens if she says "0" or "1"?

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Let p be the probability she states.
Step 2: If she says p, the statement "this statement has probability p of being true"
        should itself have probability p of being true.
Step 3: But what does "true" mean here? The statement IS the probability.
        The statement "my next statement will be true with probability p" IS her statement.

Step 4: If p = 1: She claims 100% her statement is true. Is it true that it's true
        with probability 1? Only if it's definitely true. The statement "probability 1"
        is true iff it's certain. Self-consistent.

Step 5: If p = 0: She claims 0% her statement is true. For this to be consistent,
        the statement must be false 100% of the time. If it's false that p = 0...
        then the probability is NOT 0. But then her accuracy (0%) doesn't match (p ≠ 0).
        Contradiction.

Step 6: If p = 0.5: She claims 50% true. For consistency, the statement should be
        true 50% of the time. But either the probability IS 0.5 (always) or it isn't.
        It can't sometimes be 0.5 and sometimes not.

Step 7: The only consistent answer is p = 1 (or any p where we interpret consistency
        in a specific way).

ACTUALLY: Let me reconsider. The oracle's statement is about her NEXT statement.
But this IS her next statement. So she's making a self-referential claim.

The paradox: If she says "50%", is that statement true or false?
If true: the probability is actually 50%. But then it should be true 50% of time. ??
If false: the probability is NOT 50%. What is it?

ANSWER: The question is paradoxical (similar to Liar's Paradox).
The only escape: She says "1" (100%), which is self-confirming.

- Originality: 7/10 (Liar's paradox variant)
- Cleverness: 6/10 (gets paradoxical)
- Simplicity: 7/10 (short setup)
- Solution Success: 5/10 (resolves into paradox)

TOTAL: 25/40

RIDDLE 2A3: The Polling Prisoner
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
A prisoner will be executed at noon tomorrow unless he correctly predicts, tonight,
what percentage of death row prisoners make correct predictions.

The warden tells him: "Historically, exactly 40% of prisoners correctly predict the
percentage." This is true - 40% is the actual historical rate.

THE QUESTION: What should the prisoner predict?

HINT: Your prediction affects the statistic you're predicting.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Let's say N prisoners have attempted before, with 0.4N correct.
Step 2: If prisoner predicts 40% and is correct: new stats are (0.4N + 1)/(N + 1).
        For large N, this is still ≈ 40%. His prediction of 40% is correct.

Step 3: If prisoner predicts 40% and is wrong: new stats are 0.4N/(N + 1) ≈ 40%.
        But if he's wrong, 40% isn't the right answer? Let's think harder.

Step 4: The percentage is about predicting the percentage. It's meta.
        Currently 40% predict correctly. What did they predict?
        They predicted various things. 40% happened to be right.

Step 5: What's the "correct" answer? The percentage is about correct predictions of...
        the percentage of correct predictions. Let's call the true percentage X.

Step 6: If X = 40%, then predicting "40%" is correct. You join the 40% who are correct.
        New X = (0.4N + 1)/(N + 1) ≈ 40% for large N. Consistent!

Step 7: If everyone predicted 40%: some are right, some are wrong? No - the question
        asks what percentage they predict, and if 40% is correct, all who predict 40% are right.

Wait, I'm confusing myself. Let me re-read.

"What percentage of death row prisoners make correct predictions?"
This is asking about the success rate of predictions. Currently 40% success rate.
If you predict "40%", and 40% is indeed the success rate, you're correct.

But: Your prediction affects the rate!
- If you predict 40% and you're correct: rate becomes (0.4N + 1)/(N + 1)
- If you predict 40% and you're wrong: rate becomes 0.4N/(N + 1)

For 40% to be correct after your prediction:
Need (0.4N + 1)/(N + 1) = 0.4
0.4N + 1 = 0.4N + 0.4
1 = 0.4
Contradiction!

So predicting "40%" and being correct changes the rate to ABOVE 40%.
The true answer after correct prediction is (0.4N + 1)/(N + 1) > 40%.

REVISED SOLUTION: Let r be the rate you predict.
- You're correct if r equals the new rate (after your prediction is counted).
- If correct: new rate = (0.4N + 1)/(N + 1)
- For you to be correct: r = (0.4N + 1)/(N + 1) ≈ 40.something% for large N.

But you don't know N! For large N, the change is negligible. Predict 40%.

ANSWER: Predict 40%. For sufficiently large N, this is approximately correct.

ARCHETYPE SCORES:
- Originality: 8/10 (nice self-referential statistics)
- Cleverness: 7/10 (the "your prediction affects the stat" angle)
- Simplicity: 6/10 (setup is a bit convoluted)
- Solution Success: 7/10 (answer is "approximately 40%")

TOTAL: 28/40
