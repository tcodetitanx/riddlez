RIDDLE 2B1: The Announced Auction
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
Three bidders (A, B, C) will bid for a prize worth $100 to each. Before bidding,
an announcer says: "I will now tell you something you all already know: Each of
you values this prize at $100."

Bidding is first-price sealed-bid. Highest bid wins and pays their bid.

THE QUESTION: Does the announcement change optimal bidding?

HINT: Before the announcement, each person KNEW the values. But did they know
that everyone else knew?

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Before announcement: Each knows their own value ($100). Each might not know
        if others know their values (private vs common knowledge).

Step 2: In private values auction: Each bids based on their own value, not others'.
        Optimal bid for risk-neutral bidder: shade bid below value.
        With 3 bidders, uniform distribution: bid (n-1)/n × 100 = 2/3 × 100 = $66.67.

Step 3: After announcement: Values are common knowledge. Everyone knows everyone
        values it at $100. Does this change bidding?

Step 4: In symmetric private value auction, knowing others' values shouldn't change
        your strategy (your value is what matters for your bid).

Step 5: But wait - if everyone KNOWS everyone values it equally, coordination changes.
        They might collude or compete differently.

Step 6: In a ONE-SHOT sealed bid: The Nash equilibrium is unchanged. Each bids $66.67.
        Knowing others' values doesn't help you - it only confirms symmetry.

Step 7: HOWEVER: If bidders could coordinate, they might agree to all bid $0 or $1.
        Common knowledge enables coordination that wasn't possible before.

ANSWER: In Nash equilibrium, the announcement doesn't change bids (~$67 each).
But if coordination is possible, common knowledge enables collusion.

The insight: Common knowledge doesn't change individual rationality, but it enables
coordination by making it public that everyone knows the same thing.

VERIFICATION:
- Without announcement: Each bids ~$67 (Nash equilibrium).
- With announcement: Each still bids ~$67 (same equilibrium).
- Difference: With common knowledge, they COULD coordinate to bid $1 each.
  But in one-shot Nash, they don't trust coordination.

- Simplicity: 8/10 (clear setup)

RIDDLE 2B2: The Transparent Standoff
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
Three gunfighters (A, B, C) are in a standoff. Each will shoot at one target.
- A is a perfect shot (always hits)
- B hits 80% of the time
- C hits 50% of the time

If shot, you die. Each wants to survive. They shoot simultaneously. All three
know all accuracies (common knowledge).

THE QUESTION: Who should each person target?

HINT: Being dangerous makes you a target.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: A is most dangerous (100%), then B (80%), then C (50%).
Step 2: A should shoot the biggest threat to A. That's B (80% accurate).
Step 3: B should shoot the biggest threat to B. That's A (100% accurate).
Step 4: C should shoot the biggest threat to C. That's A (100% accurate).

Step 5: Equilibrium: A shoots B, B shoots A, C shoots A.
        - A is targeted by both B and C.
        - P(A survives) = P(B misses) × P(C misses) = 0.2 × 0.5 = 0.1 = 10%
        - P(B survives) = P(A misses) = 0 (A never misses). B dies.
        - P(C survives) = 1 (nobody shoots C). C survives 100%!

Step 6: Wait - this isn't stable! If A knows C survives 100%, A might shoot C?
        No - A's survival depends on removing B. If A shoots C, B still has 80% to hit A.

Step 7: Can C do better? C could shoot B instead of A.
        - If C shoots B and hits (50%): B might die (if A also shoots B).
        - But C's survival is already 100% by not being targeted.
        - Shooting A increases A's chance of dying from 90% to 95% (marginal).
        - Shooting B instead: If B dies, A shoots... C? No, A still prefers B dead.

REVISED: Let's think about what happens:
- A's best target: B (80% threat)
- B's best target: A (100% threat)
- C's best target: A (100% threat)

But C has 50% hit rate. Even if C shoots A and misses, B also shoots A.
P(A dies) = P(B hits OR C hits) = 1 - (1-0.8)(1-0.5) = 1 - 0.1 = 0.9.
A dies 90%. A shoots B, B dies 100%.
C lives 100% (untargeted).

C IS THE WINNER by being the least threatening!

ANSWER: A shoots B, B shoots A, C shoots A. C survives with probability 1.

INSIGHT: Being the weakest is an advantage - no one bothers to shoot you.

VERIFICATION:
- A targets B (biggest threat that could kill A). ✓
- B targets A (certain to kill B if alive). ✓
- C targets A (certain to kill C if alive). ✓
- C is never targeted (too weak to be worth a bullet). ✓

ARCHETYPE SCORES:
- Originality: 6/10 (truel is classic, but common knowledge angle is explicit)
- Cleverness: 8/10 (the "weakest wins" insight is delightful)
- Simplicity: 9/10 (clear setup)
- Solution Success: 9/10 (verified equilibrium)

TOTAL: 32/40

RIDDLE 2B3: The Knowledge Cascade
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
Five people (A, B, C, D, E) sit in a circle. Each has a secret number (1-5, all
different, randomly assigned). Each can see their two neighbors' numbers.

A moderator announces: "Everyone with a number greater than their left neighbor
knows they have a number greater than their left neighbor."

This is obviously true (tautology). But then everyone starts deducing things.

THE QUESTION: After the announcement, who (if anyone) can determine their own number?

HINT: The announcement converts mutual knowledge into common knowledge.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Before announcement: Each person sees two numbers. Five positions with
        numbers 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 distributed.

Step 2: Person with 1 knows neighbors have larger numbers. Person with 5 knows neighbors
        have smaller numbers.

Step 3: The announcement says "if you have X and left has Y, and X > Y, you know X > Y."
        This adds NO information directly (everyone already knows their relative sizes).

Step 4: What changes? COMMON KNOWLEDGE that everyone reasons the same way.

Step 5: Example arrangement: A=3, B=1, C=5, D=2, E=4 (around circle).
        - A sees E=4 (left) and B=1 (right). A's number (3) < left (4) and > right (1).
        - A knows: I'm not 5 (since someone to my left is 4, I could be 5, but 5 > 4 so
          if I were 5, I'd know I'm greater than left... which is tautological).

Step 6: This is getting complex. Let me consider what the 1-holder knows.
        Person with 1: Both neighbors are larger. They know their number is the smallest
        among the three visible (self + two neighbors). But they can't be sure they have 1
        unless they can deduce the full arrangement.

Step 7: After announcement + common reasoning:
        - The person with 1 can deduce they have 1 (smallest possible, both neighbors larger).
        - They already knew this before the announcement!

Actually, I realize: the announcement doesn't help here because it's tautological.
"If X > Y, you know X > Y" adds no new information.

The REAL common knowledge puzzles (like Green Eyes) announce something that IS already
known individually but becomes COMMONLY known.

This puzzle needs revision to work properly.

ANSWER: This puzzle as constructed doesn't work - the announcement is vacuous.
