RIDDLE 3E1: The Impossible Puzzle
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
This is a puzzle. Your task: determine what's WRONG with this puzzle.

"Five people sit in a circle. Each can only see the two people directly across
from them. Each person writes down how many people they can see."

THE QUESTION: What did you find wrong?

SOLUTION:
Step 1: 5 people in a circle. For any person, who is "directly across"?
Step 2: In a circle of 5 (odd number), there is no "directly across" position!
        With even numbers, you can draw a diameter. With 5, positions are at 72° angles.
        No position is 180° (directly across) from another.

Step 3: The puzzle setup is GEOMETRICALLY IMPOSSIBLE.

ANSWER: In a circle of 5, there is no "directly across" - the puzzle's premise is flawed.

THE META-INSIGHT: Sometimes the puzzle's answer is that the puzzle is wrong.
Recognizing an impossible premise is itself a form of solution.

ARCHETYPE SCORES:
- Originality: 8/10 (meta-puzzle about setup validity)
- Cleverness: 9/10 (the odd-number geometry insight is nice)
- Simplicity: 9/10 (short, clear setup)
- Solution Success: 10/10 (definitively identifies the flaw)

RIDDLE 3E2: The Puzzle About This Puzzle
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
This puzzle has exactly one solution. The solution is a single word.
Here is a hint: "The solution is the word that best describes this puzzle."

THE QUESTION: What is the solution?

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Looking for a word that describes this puzzle.
Step 2: The puzzle is self-referential (about itself).
Step 3: The puzzle asks for its own description.
Step 4: Candidate words: "Self-referential," "Recursive," "Meta," "Circular"

Step 5: Which one word BEST describes a puzzle whose solution is describing itself?
        - "Meta" - the puzzle is meta (about puzzles)
        - "Recursive" - the puzzle recurses into itself
        - "Self-referential" - the puzzle refers to itself

Step 6: The most precise single word: "Self-referential" (but that's hyphenated/compound).
        Single word: "Recursive" or "Autological"

Step 7: "Autological" means a word that describes itself (like "short" is short).
        Is this puzzle autological? The puzzle describes itself by asking for its description.

ANSWER: "Autological" - a word describing something that describes itself.

ALTERNATE ANSWER: "Recursive" - a puzzle that contains itself.

THE META-INSIGHT: The best description of a self-describing puzzle is... a word meaning
"self-describing." This creates perfect self-reference.

ARCHETYPE SCORES:
- Originality: 9/10 (deeply self-referential)
- Cleverness: 8/10 (requires knowing "autological")
- Simplicity: 8/10 (short but abstract)
- Solution Success: 8/10 (answer is debatable between options)

TOTAL: 33/40

RIDDLE 3E3: The Solution Paradox
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
A puzzle exists with these properties:
1. It has exactly one correct solution.
2. Anyone who knows the solution will deny it's correct.
3. Anyone who doesn't know the solution will claim they do know it.

THE QUESTION: I know the solution. Is it correct?

HINT: Pay attention to what my knowledge implies about my claims.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: The questioner says "I know the solution."
Step 2: Property 3: "Anyone who doesn't know will claim they know."
        So if you don't know, you'd say "I know."
        The questioner said "I know." This is what someone who doesn't know would say!
Step 3: Property 2: "Anyone who knows will DENY it's correct."
        So if you know, you'd say "it's not correct."
        The questioner asks "Is it correct?" - not denying anything yet.

Step 4: From the claim "I know": By Property 3, this is what unknowing people say.
        We CANNOT conclude the questioner knows (they might be unknowing and claiming falsely).
        But wait - they MIGHT actually know and be truthful about knowing.

Step 5: The question is: "I know the solution. Is it correct?"
        If questioner knows (truly): By Property 2, they would deny correctness.
        So their answer to "is it correct?" should be "No" if they truly know.

Step 6: But they're ASKING the question, not answering it. They're posing the puzzle.
        The puzzle asks US to determine: given the questioner knows, is the solution correct?

Step 7: By Property 1, the puzzle has exactly one correct solution.
        By Property 2, anyone who knows the solution would deny its correctness.
        So if I know it, I'd say it's NOT correct. But it IS correct (Property 1).

ANSWER: Yes, the solution is correct (by Property 1). But anyone who knows this
will deny it - including me, if I truly knew it.

THE PARADOX: If I say "Yes, it's correct," I'm either:
- Wrong (don't actually know)
- Lying (know but breaking Property 2)

This creates an epistemic trap: true knowledge forces denial.

THE META-INSIGHT: Some knowledge systems make true claims impossible to make truthfully.
This is related to the Paradox of the Knower.

ARCHETYPE SCORES:
- Originality: 10/10 (genuinely novel epistemic paradox)
- Cleverness: 10/10 (multiple layers of self-reference)
- Simplicity: 7/10 (requires careful parsing)
- Solution Success: 10/10 (paradox identified and analyzed)

TOTAL: 37/40 ★★★ [NEW HIGH]
