RIDDLE 3C1: The Last Number
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
Two players take turns saying a number from 1-10. The first player to say a
number that has been said before loses.

THE QUESTION: Who wins with perfect play, and what's the strategy?

SOLUTION:
Step 1: There are 10 unique numbers. Each turn uses up one number.
Step 2: Maximum turns = 10 (all numbers said once).
Step 3: Turn 1: Player 1 says a number. 9 remain.
        Turn 2: Player 2 says a number. 8 remain.
        ...
        Turn 10: Player 2 (if even turns) or Player 1 (if odd).

Step 4: 10 turns total. Turn 10 is... let's see:
        Turn 1 (P1), Turn 2 (P2), Turn 3 (P1), ..., Turn 10 (P2).
        Player 2 takes even turns. Turn 10 = P2.

Step 5: On turn 10, only 1 number remains (if no repeats yet). P2 says it.
        On turn 11, P1 must repeat. P1 loses.

Step 6: But wait - the game ends when someone repeats, not after 10 turns.
        If both play optimally (never repeating until forced), the 11th turn is the loss.

ANSWER: Player 1 loses. With 10 numbers and P1 starting, P2 makes the last safe move.

VERIFICATION:
- Optimal play: never repeat until forced.
- 10 unique numbers. After 10 turns, all used. Turn 11 forces a repeat.
- Turn 11 is P1's turn (odd). P1 must repeat. P1 loses. ✓

RIDDLE 3C2: The Honest Liar
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
A person says: "Everything I say is a lie."

THE QUESTION: Is this statement true or false?

SOLUTION:
Step 1: If true: Everything they say is a lie. But this statement is true. Contradiction!
Step 2: If false: Not everything they say is a lie (some statements are true).
        This statement being false means they DON'T always lie. Consistent!

Step 3: The statement is FALSE. They're lying when they say they always lie.
        They sometimes tell truth.

ANSWER: False. The speaker is lying about always lying.

INSIGHT: Self-referential statements can be consistently false but not consistently true.
         "This statement is false" = paradox.
         "Everything I say is false" = resolvable (it's false).

The difference: "This statement" is directly self-referential.
               "Everything I say" is about a set that INCLUDES this statement.

- Cleverness: 7/10 (resolution is satisfying)
- Simplicity: 10/10 (one sentence)
- Solution Success: 10/10 (clean)

RIDDLE 3C3: The Double Bind
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
You must answer this question with Yes or No. If you answer correctly, you get $100.
If you answer wrong, you lose $50.

THE QUESTION: Will you answer "No" to this question?

SOLUTION:
Step 1: If you answer "Yes": You're saying you WILL answer No. But you answered Yes.
        Contradiction! "Yes" is wrong. You lose $50.

Step 2: If you answer "No": You're saying you will NOT answer No. But you just answered No.
        Contradiction! "No" is wrong. You lose $50.

Step 3: BOTH answers are wrong. There's no winning!

ANSWER: Neither answer is correct. You lose $50 regardless.

THE INSIGHT: Some questions have no correct answer by construction. The question
creates a double bind where any response contradicts itself.

This is related to the Paradox of the Court (Protagoras paradox).

ARCHETYPE SCORES:
- Originality: 6/10 (self-referential questions are known)
- Cleverness: 8/10 (the "no winning move" insight is nice)
- Simplicity: 10/10 (ultra-clear)
- Solution Success: 10/10 (perfectly analyzed)

TOTAL: 34/40
