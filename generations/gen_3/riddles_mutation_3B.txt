RIDDLE 3B1: The Obvious Answer
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
I'm thinking of a number between 1 and 100. You get one guess.
If you guess correctly, you win $1,000,000.
If you guess wrong, you lose nothing.

Before you guess, I tell you: "The number I'm thinking of is NOT the number
that would be the optimal guess if you didn't have this information."

THE QUESTION: What number should you guess?

HINT: What would you guess without the information?

THE PARADIGM SHIFT: The optimal strategy changes because you're told about the optimal strategy.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Without extra information, every number has equal chance (1%). No guess is "better."
        But in practice, humans often guess round numbers: 50, 100, etc.

Step 2: If "optimal guess" means the number with highest probability of being correct,
        and all are equal, there's no single optimal guess. Paradox?

Step 3: Let's say the "conventional optimal" is 50 (middle number). The hint says
        the answer is NOT 50.

Step 4: Now I have 99 equally likely numbers. Still 1/99 ≈ 1% chance.
        New "optimal" guess is any of the 99. Say, 25? But if 25 becomes optimal,
        the hint should exclude 25 too!

Step 5: REGRESS: Whatever number is "optimal" gets excluded by the hint.
        This eliminates ALL numbers one by one? No, the hint was given ONCE, before your guess.

Step 6: The hint eliminated ONE number (the "optimal" guess you would have made).
        That number was some N. Now N is impossible. Pick any other.

Step 7: But which N? The puzzle says "the optimal guess if you didn't have this info."
        Without this info, all guesses are equivalent. There is no unique optimal guess.

RESOLUTION: The puzzle is paradoxical if there's no unique optimal guess.
            If we DEFINE optimal as "50" (midpoint), then answer is "not 50."
            Guess any other number; they're all equally likely (1/99).

ACTUAL INSIGHT: The information "not the optimal guess" only helps if there WAS
a unique optimal. With uniform distribution, the hint is vacuous - there was no
single optimal guess to exclude!

ANSWER: The hint provides no information. Guess any number. (Or, if 50 was your
default, now guess something else like 42.)

The paradigm shift: Sometimes "information" about optimality is useless because
there was no single optimum to begin with.

- Originality: 8/10 (self-referential optimality)
- Cleverness: 9/10 (the "no unique optimum" insight is deep)
- Simplicity: 8/10 (setup is simple)
- Solution Success: 8/10 (resolves cleanly)

RIDDLE 3B2: The Truthful Paradox
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
A being exists who can only make TRUE statements and CORRECT predictions.
This being says: "You will NOT believe this statement."

THE QUESTION: Do you believe the statement?

HINT: Consider both possibilities.

THE PARADIGM SHIFT: Some statements about beliefs are self-defeating.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: The being always speaks truth. So the statement is TRUE.
Step 2: The statement says "You will NOT believe this."
Step 3: If you believe it: The statement is false (you DO believe it). But the being
        always speaks truth. Contradiction!
Step 4: If you don't believe it: The statement is true (you don't believe it). Consistent!

Step 5: So you CANNOT believe the statement, even though it's TRUE.

ANSWER: No, you don't believe it. And that's what makes it true!

THE INSIGHT: There exist true statements that cannot be believed (because believing
them would make them false). This is related to Moore's Paradox: "It's raining but
I don't believe it's raining."

The paradigm shift: Truth and belief are not the same thing. Some truths are
unbelievable BY NECESSITY, not due to lack of evidence.

VERIFICATION:
- Statement is true (being always speaks truth) ✓
- If believed, statement becomes false ✓
- If not believed, statement remains true ✓
- Only consistent state: true and unbelieved ✓

ARCHETYPE SCORES:
- Originality: 7/10 (Moore's paradox adjacent)
- Cleverness: 9/10 (the necessary unbelievability is profound)
- Simplicity: 10/10 (one sentence puzzle)
- Solution Success: 10/10 (perfect resolution)

TOTAL: 36/40 ★★

RIDDLE 3B3: The Cooperative Traitor
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
Ten people play a game. Each privately chooses: COOPERATE or DEFECT.
- If everyone cooperates: each gets $100.
- If anyone defects: defectors get $150, cooperators get $0.

Before choosing, everyone is told: "At least one person has already committed
to defecting. You cannot change their mind."

THE QUESTION: What should you do?

HINT: Does the information change your optimal strategy?

THE PARADIGM SHIFT: Sometimes information about others' decisions is self-fulfilling.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Without the information: Cooperate if you trust others; defect if you don't.
        Classic prisoner's dilemma. Defecting is dominant strategy (always better).

Step 2: With the information: At least one person will defect.
        If someone will definitely defect: cooperating gives you $0 (cooperators get nothing).
        Defecting gives you $150.

Step 3: You should DEFECT. Cooperation is useless with a guaranteed defector.

Step 4: But wait - EVERYONE received this information! Everyone reasons: "I should defect."
        So everyone defects. Everyone gets $150? No wait...

Step 5: Re-read payoffs: "If anyone defects: defectors get $150."
        This implies if ALL defect, all get $150.

Wait, that's better than all cooperate ($100)! No, that can't be right.

REVISED PAYOFFS (standard prisoners' dilemma):
- All cooperate: $100 each
- K defect, rest cooperate: Defectors get $150, cooperators get $0
- All defect: Everyone gets $10 (mutual defection is worst for everyone)

Step 6: With revised payoffs and guaranteed defector:
        - Cooperating gives $0 (some defector exists)
        - Defecting gives $10-$150 depending on how many defect

Step 7: Since at least one defects, cooperating = $0. Defecting ≥ $10. Defect is better.
        But if everyone defects, all get $10 instead of $100 (if all cooperated).

THE INSIGHT: The announcement "at least one will defect" becomes SELF-FULFILLING.
             Before the announcement, cooperation might have been possible.
             After the announcement, defection is rational, so everyone defects.

This is the common knowledge cascade! The announcement created the defection it predicted.

ANSWER: Defect. The announcement guarantees you can't benefit from cooperation.

THE PARADIGM SHIFT: Predictions about defection can CAUSE defection, even if no one
originally planned to defect. The announcement is a self-fulfilling prophecy.

- Originality: 7/10 (game theory classic with twist)
- Cleverness: 9/10 (self-fulfilling prophecy insight)
- Simplicity: 9/10 (clear setup)
- Solution Success: 9/10 (clean resolution)
