RIDDLE 3A1: The Forgetful Gods
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
Three gods sit in a row. Each knows everything EXCEPT the answer to one specific
question (different for each god). You may ask any god any yes/no question.
However, a god's answer might be WRONG if your question is the one thing they
don't know - and they won't know they're wrong.

The gods never lie, but they may be mistaken.

You know:
- God 1 doesn't know if God 2 is ever mistaken
- God 2 doesn't know if God 3 is ever mistaken
- God 3 doesn't know if God 1 is ever mistaken

THE QUESTION: With exactly 3 questions, can you determine which god doesn't know what?

HINT: A god's answer about another god's knowledge reveals something.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Ask God 1: "Does God 2 ever give mistaken answers?"
        - God 1 doesn't know if God 2 is ever mistaken (God 1's blind spot).
        - God 1 will answer based on their lack of knowledge. They'll answer... randomly? No.
        - Since God 1 genuinely doesn't know, they can't truthfully say Yes or No.

PROBLEM: A god who doesn't know can't answer. But they "never lie" - so what do they do?

Let me reframe: A god answers their best knowledge. If their question IS their blind spot,
they give an answer that MIGHT be wrong (they believe it's true but it isn't).

Step 2: Ask God 1: "Is God 2's blind spot about God 3?"
        - God 1 KNOWS everything except info about God 2's mistakes.
        - God 1 knows God 2's blind spot. Answer: truthful.

Hmm, this is getting circular. Let me redesign.

REDESIGNED SETUP:
Each god is omniscient EXCEPT for one color they cannot perceive.
- God 1 cannot see red
- God 2 cannot see blue
- God 3 cannot see green

A ball is placed in front of them. It's one solid color.
Each god tells you what color they see:
- If the ball is their blind color, they see nothing and say "I see nothing."
- Otherwise, they correctly name the color.

THE QUESTION: The gods say: "Blue," "I see nothing," "Blue." What color is the ball?

Step 1: God 1 says "Blue." God 1 can't see red. So the ball is NOT red (God 1 sees it).
Step 2: God 2 says "Nothing." God 2 can't see blue. So the ball IS blue (God 2's blind spot).
Step 3: God 3 says "Blue." God 3 can't see green. This confirms blue (God 3 sees blue).
ANSWER: Blue.

VERIFICATION: Ball is blue. God 1 (red-blind) sees blue. God 2 (blue-blind) sees nothing.
God 3 (green-blind) sees blue. ✓

But this is too simple. Let me add complexity:

ENHANCED PUZZLE: The gods only say their responses ONCE, simultaneously. You don't know
which god has which blind spot. Given the three responses, deduce the ball's color AND
which god is blind to which color.

Responses: "Blue," "I see nothing," "Green" (from gods 1, 2, 3 in order)

Step 1: One god sees nothing → the ball IS that god's blind color.
Step 2: Two gods see colors. If they see different colors, that's impossible (one ball).
        "Blue" and "Green" are different! Contradiction?

Step 3: This means one of them is WRONG - they're seeing their non-blind color as something else?
        No, gods see correctly if not blind.

REVISED: The puzzle setup is breaking. Let me make it work:

If ball is Blue: Blue-blind god sees nothing. Others see Blue.
If responses are "Blue, Nothing, Green": Blue-seer, Blue-blind, Green-seer.
But one ball can't be both Blue and Green!

The puzzle as enhanced is broken. Original version works:

FINAL PUZZLE: Three gods, three blind spots (red, blue, green), one ball, three responses.
Given responses match the simple version, the puzzle works.

RIDDLE 3A2: The Probability Prisoner
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
You're told you'll be executed with probability P, where P is the fraction of
execution-predicters (people who predict their own execution) who are right.

You must predict: will you be executed? Your prediction affects P!

Currently, 100 people have predicted before you:
- 50 predicted "Yes" (executed), and 40 of them were right (80% accurate)
- 50 predicted "No" (not executed), and 30 of them were right (60% accurate)

THE QUESTION: What should you predict to minimize your chance of execution?

HINT: Calculate P for each prediction you could make.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: Current data: 40 correct "Yes" predictions, 30 correct "No" predictions.
        70 correct predictions total out of 100. P = 70% currently.

Step 2: If you predict "Yes" (executed):
        - If you're right: 41 correct "Yes", 30 correct "No". 71/101 = 70.3% = new P.
          You're executed with probability 70.3%.
        - If you're wrong: 40 correct "Yes", 30 correct "No". 70/101 = 69.3%.
          But you're wrong, so you're NOT executed. Contradiction? Let's think...

Step 3: This is self-referential! Your accuracy affects P, which determines your execution,
        which determines your accuracy.

Step 4: If you predict "Yes":
        - Case: You're executed. You were right. New P = 71/101 = 70.3%.
        - Case: You're not executed. You were wrong. New P = 70/101 = 69.3%.
        Your execution probability was P... but P depends on outcome. Circular!

Step 5: Fixed point: Let p = P(you're executed | you predict Yes).
        If executed (prob p): new P = 71/101, and your execution prob should equal new P? No...

Actually, let me re-read: "executed with probability P, where P is fraction correct."

So P is determined AFTER all predictions, based on accuracy. But your prediction is part of that!

SIMPLIFICATION:
Let's say P is set AFTER you make your prediction AND your fate is determined.
If you predict Yes and are executed: P = 71/101 ≈ 70.3%.
If you predict Yes and aren't executed: P = 70/101 ≈ 69.3%.

But your execution is determined BY P! Circular dependence.

RESOLUTION: The only consistent outcomes are fixed points.

Fixed point analysis:
If P = 71/101: You're executed with prob 71/101 IF that's self-consistent.
If executed: correct, contributes to numerator. P = 71/101. ✓
If not executed: incorrect, P = 70/101 ≠ 71/101. Not consistent with P = 71/101.

So IF P = 71/101, you must be executed. This is self-consistent.

ANSWER: If you predict "Yes," you will be executed (fixed point at ~70%).
        If you predict "No," what happens?

If you predict No and are not executed: correct. P = 71/101 (40+31 correct).
If you predict No and are executed: incorrect. P = 70/101 (40+30 correct).

For P = 71/101 and you predicted No, not executed: consistent!
For P = 70/101 and you predicted No, executed: is being executed at 70% consistent?
        Not deterministically. Probabilistically, 70% execution is a random outcome.

This is very complex.

SIMPLIFIED ANSWER: Predict "No" to minimize execution.
Reasoning: Predicting "No" gives you 30% survival minimum (correct "No" sayers survived).
Predicting "Yes" gives you 0% survival minimum (you're predicting death).

- Originality: 9/10 (self-referential probability is novel)
- Cleverness: 8/10 (fixed point analysis is interesting)
- Simplicity: 4/10 (very confusing)
- Solution Success: 5/10 (incomplete resolution)

RIDDLE 3A3: The Uncountable Room
------------------------------------------------------------------------
SETUP:
A room contains infinitely many boxes, one for each real number between 0 and 1.
Each box is labeled with its number and contains either a gold coin or nothing.

You may open any finite number of boxes. You win if at least one opened box has gold.

A demon tells you: "Exactly half of the boxes have gold. The gold boxes are distributed
so that in any interval [a, b], the fraction with gold is exactly 1/2."

THE QUESTION: Can you guarantee finding gold, and if so, how?

HINT: Think about measure theory.

SOLUTION:
Step 1: There are uncountably many boxes. You can open finitely many.
Step 2: "Half have gold" and "every interval has half gold" - this is the uniform
        distribution of gold over [0,1].
Step 3: Probability of gold in any single box: Well, each box is a single point.
        Points have measure zero in continuous distributions. P(gold in specific box) = ?

Step 4: Actually, "exactly half have gold" means the set of gold boxes has measure 1/2.
        "Every interval has half gold" means the density is 1/2 everywhere.

Step 5: If you pick any specific box (say, box 0.5), the probability it has gold is...
        actually undefined at points. But we can say: pick randomly from [0,1],
        probability = 1/2.

Step 6: But you're not picking randomly - you're picking specifically!
        If you open box 0.5, either it has gold or it doesn't. The demon's statement
        gives you no information about SPECIFIC boxes, only about intervals.

Step 7: ANY finite set of boxes has measure zero. The demon's guarantee about intervals
        says nothing about finite sets of points.

ANSWER: You CANNOT guarantee finding gold. Opening any finite number of boxes gives
        no guarantee, because a measure-1/2 set can completely avoid any given finite set.

VERIFICATION:
Consider a specific construction: gold is in [0, 0.5] only.
This satisfies "half have gold" but NOT "every interval has half gold."

With uniform density, opening boxes at 0.1, 0.2, ..., 0.9 gives expected 4.5 gold boxes,
but no guarantee (bad luck is possible, though vanishingly unlikely).

The puzzle explores the limits of probability with infinite sets.
