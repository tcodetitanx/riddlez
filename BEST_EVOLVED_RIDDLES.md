# The Perfect Puzzle (40/40) ★★★★
**Generation 4, Riddle 7**

### The Puzzle
What question has every answer be correct?

### The Answer
"What could you answer to this question?"

### The Insight
Whatever you answer IS what you could answer—by definition, your response proves itself correct. This creates a question where every possible answer validates itself, a genuinely novel puzzle structure.

### Verification
- If you answer "Apple": Could you answer "Apple"? Yes, you just did. ✓
- If you answer "42": Could you answer "42"? Yes. ✓
- If you answer nothing: Could you answer nothing? Yes. ✓

# The Only Question (39/40) ★★★
**Generation 4, Riddle 1**

### The Puzzle
What question, when answered truthfully, can only be answered "No"?

### The Answer
"Is this question unanswerable?"

### The Insight
By answering the question at all, you prove it's answerable—so the truthful answer must be "No." The act of responding refutes any claim that the question cannot be answered.

### Verification
If you say "Yes" (it's unanswerable), you just answered it—contradiction.
If you say "No" (it's answerable), you're correct—you just proved it.

# The Solution Paradox (37/40) ★★★
**Generation 3, Riddle 3E3**

### The Puzzle
A puzzle exists with these properties:
1. It has exactly one correct solution.
2. Anyone who knows the solution will deny it's correct.
3. Anyone who doesn't know the solution will claim they know it.

I know the solution. Is it correct?

### The Answer
Yes, the solution is correct (by Property 1). But anyone who truly knows this—including the questioner—must deny it.

### The Insight
This creates an epistemic trap: true knowledge of the solution forces you to deny it. You cannot truthfully say "I know the solution and it's correct" because knowing it compels denial.

# The Truthful Paradox (36/40) ★★
**Generation 3, Riddle 3B2**

### The Puzzle
A being who can only make true statements says: "You will not believe this statement."

### The Answer
You do not believe it—and that's what makes it true.

### The Insight
This is a true statement that cannot be believed. If you believe it, it becomes false. Some truths are unbelievable by necessity, not due to lack of evidence.

# The Impossible Puzzle (36/40) ★★
**Generation 3, Riddle 3E1**

### The Puzzle
Five people sit in a circle. Each can only see the two people directly across from them. Each person writes down how many people they can see.

What's wrong with this puzzle?

### The Answer
In a circle of 5 (odd number), there is no "directly across" position. The puzzle's premise is geometrically impossible.

### The Insight
Sometimes the puzzle's answer is that the puzzle itself is flawed. Recognizing an impossible premise is a valid form of solution.

# The Self-Fulfilling Survey (35/40) ★
**Generation 2, Riddle 2A1**

### The Puzzle
1000 people are asked: "Will more than 500 people answer 'Yes' to this question?"

### The Answer
Two equilibria exist: either everyone says "Yes" (making it true) or everyone says "No" (making it true that ≤500 said Yes). The question creates its own answer through collective belief.

### The Insight
Self-referential predictions can have multiple stable outcomes. The truth depends on what everyone expects everyone else to do.

# The Clone Dilemma (35/40) ★
**Generation 0 (Original), Puzzle 8**

### The Puzzle
You'll be cloned 99 times. Each clone decides independently to push a button. If more than 50 push, all 100 die. If 50 or fewer push, pushers get $1M each.

### The Answer
Don't push. All 100 copies will reason identically, so you're not deciding for yourself—you're deciding a policy for all copies. Pushing means all push means all die.

### The Insight
Perfect copies make identical decisions. "What should I do?" becomes "What policy should exist?" since all copies will reach the same conclusion.

# The Recursive Election (34/40)
**Generation 2, Riddle 2E2**

### The Puzzle
Voters' preferences depend on their prediction of who will win. Those predicting the winner vote for them; those predicting the loser vote against; those predicting second place vote for that candidate.

### The Answer
Three-way tie. Any prediction of a winner causes votes to redistribute, destabilizing that outcome. The only stable outcome is a tie.

### The Insight
Self-referential voting systems can collapse into equilibria where predictions become self-defeating, forcing symmetric outcomes.

# The Double Bind (34/40)
**Generation 3, Riddle 3C3**

### The Puzzle
You must answer Yes or No: "Will you answer 'No' to this question?"

### The Answer
Neither answer is correct. Both "Yes" and "No" create contradictions—you lose regardless.

### The Insight
Some questions are designed to have no correct answer. The solution is recognizing the trap, not escaping it.
